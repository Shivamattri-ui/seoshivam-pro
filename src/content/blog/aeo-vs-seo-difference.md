In almost every new client conversation, the same question appears early: "should we do SEO or AEO?" The framing assumes they are alternatives. In my experience across 30+ brands in the US, UK, EU, and Australia, the brands that treat them as separate decisions consistently underperform the brands that build one system for both surfaces.

[AEO (Answer Engine Optimization)](/insights/what-is-aeo) and SEO (Search Engine Optimization) are not competing strategies. They are complementary disciplines targeting different parts of the same buyer journey. The choice is not which one to do. It is understanding the difference well enough to sequence them correctly. As an [AEO and SEO specialist](/), I build both from a single architecture.

## The core difference in one sentence

SEO gets you ranked in Google's list of ten blue links. AEO gets you cited in the single AI-generated answer that appears before those links.

## What SEO targets

Traditional SEO optimizes for <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/how-search-works" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">Google's ranking algorithm</a>. The key variables:

- **Backlinks**: External sites linking to yours, weighted by their own authority
- **On-page relevance**: Correct use of target keywords in title, headers, and body
- **Technical factors**: Page speed, mobile-friendliness, indexation, crawlability
- **Content quality**: Depth, accuracy, and user engagement signals

When SEO works, your page appears in positions 1 through 10 for a target query. The user sees your title and description alongside competitors. They choose whether to click.

The strength of SEO: durable, compounding traffic at scale when the right architecture is in place.
The weakness of SEO: the "click" stage. You are one option in a list. Users compare.

## What AEO targets

AEO optimizes for AI engines' synthesis algorithms. The key variables:

- **Entity recognition**: Your brand is a defined entity in the AI knowledge graph
- **Structured data**: [JSON-LD schema markup](/insights/schema-markup-for-ai-search) that makes your content machine-readable
- **Answer format**: Content written as direct answers, not narrative paragraphs
- **Topical authority**: Deep coverage of a specific niche, not broad general content

When AEO works, AI engines cite your brand when synthesizing responses. The user gets an answer and sees your brand name as the source. They often visit your site already trusting you.

The strength of AEO: pre-qualified trust. Users who arrive from AI citations already believe you are an authority.
The weakness of AEO: longer citation cycles and harder-to-track attribution.

## Where they overlap

Both SEO and AEO benefit from:

- **High-quality content**: Thin, inaccurate, or unhelpful content performs poorly in both
- **Technical foundations**: Fast load times, correct indexation, and crawlable architecture
- **Topical depth**: Comprehensive coverage of a niche outperforms isolated keyword targeting
- **Domain authority**: Trusted domains earn both rankings and citations more easily

Building for AEO rarely hurts SEO. Building for SEO alone often leaves AEO unaddressed.

## Where they diverge

The clearest divergence is in content format.

**SEO-optimized content** often includes long introductions, keyword-rich subheadings, and comprehensive coverage that builds toward a conclusion. The goal is to demonstrate content depth and keep users on the page.

**AEO-optimized content** leads with the direct answer in the first two sentences. Every section starts with its answer. The format matches how AI engines extract and synthesize information.

The divergence in schema markup is equally significant. SEO uses schema primarily for rich snippets in Google search results. AEO uses schema to signal entity relationships, question-answer pairs, and content type directly to AI engines.

A well-built page does both: structured data that generates rich snippets in traditional search AND signals to AI engines that the content directly answers specific questions.

## Do you need both?

Yes. Here is why.

The search landscape in 2025 is split. A significant portion of lower-funnel research queries now happen in AI engines before Google. "What is the best X for Y" queries increasingly start in <a href="https://openai.com" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">ChatGPT</a> or <a href="https://www.perplexity.ai" rel="nofollow noreferrer" target="_blank">Perplexity</a>. But "how to do X" and "X vs Y" queries still heavily favor traditional search.

A brand visible only in AI engines misses the significant search traffic still flowing through Google. A brand visible only in Google misses the growing pool of buyers whose first research touchpoint is an AI engine.

The brands building durable organic growth right now are building both simultaneously. SEO architecture that compounds over time. AEO implementation that earns AI citations. One integrated system, not two separate workstreams.

## How to prioritize if you are starting from zero

### Start with technical SEO and AEO simultaneously.
The technical foundations largely overlap: crawlable site, correct indexation, fast load times, XML sitemap. Do this once, correctly, and it serves both disciplines.

Then:

1. Implement entity schema (serves AEO primarily)
2. Fix any robots.txt issues blocking AI bots (serves AEO) — use the [robots.txt checker](/tools/robots-txt-checker) to audit in seconds
3. Build your first [topical content cluster](/insights/topical-authority-saas-playbook) (serves both)
4. Add FAQPage schema to service and product pages (serves AEO primarily)
5. Build backlinks through content outreach (serves SEO primarily)

**If you have to choose one to start first**: fix your robots.txt and add entity schema. It takes two hours and immediately opens the door to AI citation. Everything else can layer in over time.

## The honest reality

Most brands treating AEO and SEO as separate budget decisions are making a mistake. The incremental cost of adding AEO to an existing SEO engagement is low. The incremental benefit, AI search visibility reaching buyers before they ever hit Google, is significant and growing.

The question is not whether to do both. The question is in what order and at what pace.
